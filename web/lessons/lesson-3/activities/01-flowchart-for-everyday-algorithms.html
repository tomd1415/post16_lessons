a01 - Flowchart for Everyday Algorithms

How this works: This activity is broken into small steps with quick checks and spaces to add your own ideas.

Step 1: Quick checks

Prompt 1: Which flowchart symbol represents a decision?

Prompt 2: A sequence in an algorithm means...

Prompt 3: Pseudocode is best described as...

Step 2: Key ideas in your own words

Explain the main idea of Flowchart for Everyday Algorithms in one or two sentences.

Step 3: Build the main task

Learning objective: Practice with flowchart symbols

Suggested use: Development of a student activity on algorithms

Understand the decision symbol

First attempts at everyday algorithms tend to represent linear sequences. Most

recipes are a step by step sequence, with perhaps a 'wait for oven to heat up'

being the furthest deviation from the sequential flow. Most instructions for putting

furniture together are step by step.

Encourage the students to find ways that their everyday algorithms could contain

an 'if' scenario, e.g.

How does the getting ready for school algorithm change if there is a power

cut, or there is no cereal for breakfast?

How does a sandwich recipe change depending on what is available in the

fridge?

How do the furniture assembly instructions change if you've made a

mistake in assembly?

Step 4: Reflection

Encourage pupils to note what was easy, what was tricky, and what they would change.

Teacher guidance (answer prompts)

  • Quick check 1: Diamond
  • Quick check 2: instructions followed in order
  • Quick check 3: structured plain-language steps
  • Learning objective focus: Practice with flowchart symbols
  • Look for clear steps and correct use of key terms.
